Course Description

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to business research methods for graduate students in MBA and MSBA programs. Students learn to formulate research problems, select appropriate research designs, collect data using qualitative and quantitative methods, and analyze results to support evidence-based business decision-making. The course emphasizes both the theoretical foundations and practical applications of research methodology, with an integrated focus on ethical research practices grounded in the Jesuit tradition.

Course Objectives

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. Translate a management problem into a feasible research question with clear objectives and testable hypotheses
  2. Select and justify an appropriate research design (exploratory, descriptive, or causal) for a given business problem
  3. Evaluate secondary data sources and conduct a structured literature review
  4. Design and critique qualitative research studies, including focus groups, interviews, and observational methods
  5. Construct valid and reliable survey instruments with proper measurement scales
  6. Develop sampling plans that balance rigor, feasibility, and cost
  7. Apply fundamental statistical tests (chi-square, t-tests, ANOVA) and interpret their results in a business context
  8. Prepare professional research reports that communicate findings and strategic recommendations
  9. Integrate ethical considerations into every stage of the research process

Required Readings

There is no single required textbook. Instead, this course provides comprehensive module content supplemented by readings from the following sources, which are recommended for deeper study:

  • Zikmund, W. G., Babin, B. J., Carr, J. C., & Griffin, M. (2013). Business Research Methods (9th ed.). Cengage Learning.
  • Churchill, G. A., & Iacobucci, D. (2015). Marketing Research: Methodological Foundations. Cengage Learning.
  • Patil, V. H. (2024). Notes on Survey Design. Online.

Additional journal articles, reports, and online resources are recommended within each module.

Weekly Schedule

Week 1: The Strategic Role of Research in Business

Topics: What is business research? The research process. Characteristics of quality information. Basic vs. applied research. In-house vs. external research. Introduction to ethics.

Deliverables: Introduction discussion post; Module 1 reflection

Week 2: Defining Research Problems and Hypotheses

Topics: Sources of research problems. Problem formulation process. Decision statements and research objectives. Hypothesis development. The research request. Key questions at each stage.

Deliverables: Problem formulation exercise; Discussion post

Week 3: Research Design

Topics: Exploratory, descriptive, and causal research designs. Internal and external validity. Threats to validity. Laboratory vs. field experiments. Matching designs to data collection methods.

Deliverables: Research design critique; Discussion post

View Module →

Week 4: Secondary Data and Literature Review

Topics: Primary vs. secondary data. Internal and external secondary sources. Evaluating data quality. Business databases and digital analytics. Conducting a literature review.

Deliverables: Secondary data evaluation exercise; Annotated bibliography (5 sources)

Week 5: Qualitative Research Methods

Topics: Qualitative vs. quantitative research. Case studies and pilot studies. Depth interviews. Focus groups. Projective techniques. Observation methods. Limitations and emerging approaches.

Week 6: Survey Research and Questionnaire Design

Topics: Survey methods and errors. Measurement and scaling fundamentals. Attitude measurement. Questionnaire construction. Question wording. Pretesting. Survey modes.

Week 7: Sampling Design and Data Collection

Topics: Census vs. sampling. Probability and non-probability methods. Stratified and cluster sampling. Sample size determination. Sampling errors and bias. Data collection management.

Academic Integrity

All students are expected to adhere to Gonzaga University's Academic Integrity Policy. In a course focused on research methods, integrity is not merely a policy requirement but a professional imperative. Plagiarism, fabrication of data, or misrepresentation of sources undermines the entire purpose of evidence-based inquiry. If you are uncertain about proper attribution, ask.
